The Complete Tree Frog Care Guide

Tree frogs for sale at The Reptile Plug represent some of the most visually appealing and beginner-friendly amphibians available. Our collection includes several popular species, each with specific care requirements that, when properly met, result in thriving, long-lived pets.

White's Tree Frog Care

Whites tree frogs, also known as dumpy tree frogs, are among the most popular frogs for sale due to their docile temperament and ease of care. These chunky, green amphibians can live 15-20 years in captivity, making them long-term companions. When you buy frogs online from our collection, each White's tree frog comes with our complete species-specific care guide.

White's tree frogs require a vertical 20-gallon enclosure minimum, with plenty of branches and cork bark for climbing and perching. These animals are not tree frogs in the traditional sense—they're more accurately "perching frogs" that spend daylight hours stationary on branches. Humidity should be maintained between 60-80%, achieved through daily misting. Temperature should remain between 75-80°F during the day, with a slight drop at night.

These amphibians are voracious feeders, consuming crickets, roaches, and grasshoppers. Juvenile frogs should be fed daily, while adults can be fed every 2-3 days. Calcium supplementation is crucial for females, particularly during breeding season. An under-tank heater or ceramic heat emitter maintains proper temperature in cooler climates.

Red Eye Tree Frog Husbandry

Red-eyed tree frogs are the iconic rainforest frog and among the most beautiful frogs for sale. Their brilliant green coloration, white flanks, and striking red eyes make them instantly recognizable. However, these frogs are considerably more delicate than White's tree frogs and are best suited for experienced keepers, though not impossible for dedicated beginners.

Red eye tree frogs require taller enclosures—a 40-gallon breeder tank or similar (36"h x 18"w x 18"d) is ideal for a small group. These arboreal amphibians spend virtually all time above ground, requiring abundant live plants and vertical structures. Humidity must be maintained at 80-90%, necessitating electronic humidifiers or specialized enclosure designs. Temperature should stay between 75-80°F.

These frogs are sticky-tongued predators that feed on flying insects. Fruit flies, gnats, moths, and small crickets constitute their natural diet. They rarely descend to consume stationary prey, making live flying insects essential. Feeding 3-4 times weekly usually suffices for adult frogs.

Amazon Milk Frog and Specialty Tree Frogs

Amazon milk frogs, with their distinctive blue-gray coloration and robust build, occupy a middle ground between White's tree frogs and Red eye tree frogs in terms of care difficulty. These nocturnal amphibians can be grouped and are excellent for keepers seeking slightly more exotic tree frogs for sale than the common species.

Other specialty tree frogs we offer include the Mossy Tree Frog, with its moss-like texture and cryptic coloration; the Waxy Monkey Frog, notable for its unusual waxy secretions that reduce water loss; and the ethereal Glass Frog, whose translucent undersides reveal internal organs. Dart Frog Vivarium Setup: Creating a Poison Dart Frog Paradise

Dart frogs for sale represent pinnacle amphibian pet opportunities—colorful, interactive, and well-suited to naturalistic vivarium designs. While commonly called "poison dart frogs," captive-bred specimens lose toxicity due to different diets. However, their brilliant coloration—blues, reds, oranges, and complex patterns—makes them among the most stunning animals available.

Species Selection Among Dart Frogs

Our selection of dart frogs for sale includes multiple color morphs and species. Blue poison dart frogs display cobalt blue coloration with black reticulation. Strawberry poison dart frogs range from brilliant red to orange, often with black spots. Green and black poison dart frogs showcase emerald green bodies with black markings. Bumble bee dart frogs display yellow and black coloration reminiscent of their namesake. Dyeing poison dart frogs feature variable coloration from red to blue within single populations.

For first-time dart frog keepers, we recommend starting with Dendrobates tinctorius (dyeing poison dart frogs) or Dendrobates auratus (green and black poison dart frogs). These species are hardy, breed readily in captivity, and tolerate slight environmental variations. Blue poison dart frogs, while more stunning, require more precise conditions.

Vivarium Construction and Substrate

Successful dart frog vivaria begin with proper enclosure dimensions. Minimum 10-gallon tanks house 2-3 frogs, though 20-gallon long tanks (30"x12"x12") provide superior space and aesthetics. Vertical space is less critical than for tree frogs—dart frogs spend significant time on the forest floor—but height should be at least 12 inches.

Substrate forms the foundation of a healthy dart frog vivarium. A proper substrate should retain moisture while preventing bacterial growth. Many keepers use a two-layer system: a bottom drainage layer of hydro balls or cork bark pieces, topped with 2-3 inches of sphagnum moss mixed with coco husk and leaf litter. This combination mimics forest floor conditions, facilitates beneficial microorganism growth, and supports plant life.

Live plants are not mere decoration in dart frog vivaria—they're essential. Plants provide climbing surfaces, create visual barriers reducing aggression, moderate humidity, and support microfauna that dart frogs consume. Beginner-friendly plants include Pothos, Philodendron, Ficus pumila, and moisture-loving bromeliads. The resulting ecosystem becomes partially self-sustaining, with springtails and mites colonizing the substrate and becoming supplemental nutrition.

Humidity, Temperature, and Lighting

Dart frogs require consistent humidity between 80-90%, maintained through daily misting or automated foggers. Temperature should remain between 70-75°F—notably cooler than many amphibians. Avoid direct heat lamps; instead, use ambient room temperature or under-tank heating pads set to low settings. LED grow lights provide appropriate lighting that supports plant growth without generating excessive heat.

Feeding Dart Frogs

Dart frogs consume arthropods considerably smaller than those preferred by larger frogs. Fruit flies, spring tails, and pinhead crickets constitute their primary diet. Many keepers maintain fruit fly cultures, creating sustainable food production. Small mealworms, wax worms (sparingly), and aphids supplement the diet. Feeding frequency varies by age—juveniles should eat daily, while adults thrive on feeding 3-4 times weekly.

The Complete Axolotl Care Guide: Caring for These Aquatic Salamanders

Axolotls for sale represent one of the most unique and compelling aquatic pets available. These permanently aquatic salamanders, native to Mexico but extinct in the wild, are captive-bred in various color morphs. When you buy frogs online or rather axolotls for sale from The Reptile Plug, you're acquiring an animal that can live 12-15 years with proper care.

Understanding Axolotl Morphs

Axolotls for sale come in several distinct color morphs. Wild type axolotls display dark brown to black coloration with metallic sheen, replicating their natural appearance. Leucistic axolotls are pale pink or white with dark eyes, representing the most recognizable axolotl morph. Melanoid axolotls are solid black, lacking the iridescent sheen of wild type specimens. We offer all three primary morphs, each equally healthy and equally suitable as pet salamanders.

Tank Setup and Filtration

Despite common misconceptions, axolotls require more space than many assume. A 20-gallon long tank (30"x12"x12") is the minimum for a single axolotl, with an additional 10 gallons per additional animal. These salamanders are sensitive to water quality and appreciate established, cycled tanks. Unlike fish tanks that cycle in weeks, properly seeding an axolotl tank with media from an established aquarium accelerates colonization of beneficial bacteria.

Filtration is critical but must be gentle. Axolotls dislike strong currents and can become stressed by powerful filters. A sponge filter or hang-on-back filter with flow directed gently through the tank provides adequate filtration without creating turbulence. Water should be changed 20% weekly in established tanks, or 50% biweekly in newly cycled setups.

Substrate selection is important—avoid gravel that could cause impaction if ingested. Fine sand or bare bottom tanks work best. If using sand, select very fine aquarium sand, not play sand. Decorations should be smooth or have no sharp edges, as axolotls' delicate skin abrades easily. PVC pipes, large smooth rocks, and aquatic plants provide enrichment.

Water Parameters and Temperature

Perhaps the most critical aspect of axolotl care is temperature. These salamanders are cold-water animals, thriving between 60-64°F. Temperatures above 74°F cause stress and promote fungal infections. In warm climates, axolotls require aquarium chillers—a significant investment but absolutely necessary. Conversely, many basement or unheated room setups provide ideal conditions naturally.

Water parameters should be neutral to slightly alkaline (pH 6.5-8.0), with zero ammonia and nitrite. Nitrates should be kept below 20 ppm through regular water changes. Hardness between 6-8 dGH provides ideal conditions. Testing water regularly, especially in new setups, prevents common problems. Many axolotl keepers test weekly for the first month, then monthly once established.

Feeding and Diet

Axolotls are carnivorous and voracious predators. In the wild, they consume fish, insect larvae, and other salamanders. In captivity, axolotls readily accept earthworms, bloodworms, brine shrimp, and various aquatic invertebrates. Many keepers feed a mix—earthworms 2-3 times weekly, supplemented with freeze-dried or frozen bloodworms. Juveniles should eat daily, while adults thrive on feeding every other day.

Avoid feeding live fish to axolotls. While natural, fish introduce parasites and diseases. Additionally, some fish species possess spines or barbs that cause internal injuries. Pre-killed or captive-raised feeder invertebrates eliminate these risks.

Handling and Temperament

Despite appearing docile, axolotls are surprisingly sensitive to handling. Their delicate skin is easily damaged, and stress from repeated handling can shorten lifespans. Observe your axolotls regularly, but reserve hands-on interaction for necessary tank maintenance. If handling is necessary, wet your hands and work gently. Many axolotls, particularly leucistic morphs, recognize their keepers and demonstrate anticipatory behavior during feeding time.

Other Salamanders and Newts: Beyond Axolotls

While axolotls dominate the salamander category of frogs and salamanders for sale, terrestrial and semi-aquatic species offer diverse keeping experiences.

Tiger Salamanders and Spotted Salamanders

Tiger salamanders are North American terrestrial salamanders displaying yellow or orange stripes on dark backgrounds. Adults reach 6-8 inches and live 15+ years. These amphibians require 10-gallon enclosures minimum, with substrate that retains moisture (potting soil mixed with sphagnum moss). Temperature between 55-65°F maintains health—room temperature in basements or unheated spaces works well. Feed crickets and small roaches 2-3 times weekly.

Spotted salamanders are similar in requirements but prefer slightly more moisture and are more social, tolerating groups in larger enclosures. Both species appreciate live plants and hiding spots created with cork bark.

Fire Salamanders and Marbled Salamanders

Fire salamanders, with brilliant orange or yellow markings on glossy black skin, are among the most striking terrestrial salamanders. These European amphibians prefer cooler temperatures (50-60°F) and higher moisture. Marbled salamanders display intricate white or silver reticulation on dark backgrounds and are smaller, reaching 3-4 inches at maturity.

Newts: Small But Sophisticated Amphibians

Newts represent smaller amphibians requiring correspondingly smaller enclosures. Fire-bellied newts, with orange-red undersides and dark backs, are among the hardiest newts available and excellent for beginners. Alpine newts display olive-brown coloration with red bellies and white spots. Iranian Kaiser newts are among the most aquatic newts, preferring densely planted aquatic setups. Marbled newts showcase intricate patterns and are excellent for established keepers.

Newts require enclosures of at least 10 gallons for 2-3 individuals, with both aquatic and terrestrial zones. A shallow aquarium with an above-water area created with cork bark and potting soil meets their needs. Feeding involves small arthropods—bloodworms, daphnia, and small insects. Newts live 12-20 years, making them long-term commitments.

Amphibian Feeding: Nutrition and Arthropod Sourcing

Nutrition represents perhaps the most variable aspect of amphibian keeping across species. Unlike reptiles with more standardized prey, amphibians demand arthropods scaled to their size and suited to their hunting methods.

Staple Feeder Insects

Crickets are the most commonly available and widely accepted feeder insect. Sizes range from pinhead crickets (for dart frogs and small amphibians) to standard 1/2-inch and full-size crickets (for larger frogs and toads). Crickets are relatively nutritious, though gut-loading (feeding high-quality food to crickets before offering them to amphibians) enhances their nutritional value.

Roaches have become increasingly popular, particularly dubias and dubia hybrids. These arthropods are more nutritious than crickets, reproduce readily in captivity, and lack the strong odor that makes cricket colonies challenging. Many keepers maintain roach colonies specifically for amphibian feeding.

Earthworms and nightcrawlers suit amphibians preferring larger prey. Frogs and toads snap these up readily, and they're exceptionally nutritious. Sourcing from local bait shops or maintaining cultures reduces cost and ensures consistent availability.

Specialized Prey: Fruit Flies and Springtails

Dart frogs, small tree frogs, and young amphibians require tiny prey. Fruit fly cultures are straightforward to establish and maintain. Using non-flightless strains adds exercise and stimulation, though flightless varieties simplify feeding in enclosed spaces. Springtails naturally colonize bioactive vivaria but can be purchased as cultures to establish populations quickly.

Supplementation and Gut-Loading

Calcium supplementation is critical for female amphibians, particularly those destined for breeding. Calcium powder dusted onto prey before feeding ensures adequate dietary calcium. Many keepers supplement 2-3 times weekly for females and juveniles, with less frequency for adult males.

Gut-loading prey—feeding nutritious food to feeder insects before offering them to amphibians—dramatically improves nutritional transfer. Cricket gut-loading mixes containing vegetables, grains, and calcium fortification significantly enhance the nutritional profile of consumed insects.

Humidity and Habitat: Creating Natural Environments

Perhaps more than any other reptile group, amphibians depend critically on humidity and naturalistic habitat design. Understanding these requirements transforms amphibian keeping from basic pet maintenance to immersive ecological recreation.

Understanding Amphibian Humidity Requirements

Amphibians breathe partially through their skin—a process called cutaneous respiration. Dry skin impedes this critical function, causing stress and disease. Water loss through skin also occurs passively, requiring environmental humidity to prevent dehydration. Different species have evolved different humidity tolerances: tree frogs occupying rainforest canopies require 80-90% humidity, while terrestrial salamanders thriving on forest floors tolerate 70-80%.

Humidity is measured using hygrometers, which display relative humidity percentages. Analog hygrometers are inexpensive but often inaccurate; digital models provide reliable readings. Placing hygrometers at different enclosure levels reveals humidity gradients—important for species that exploit microclimates.

Achieving Target Humidity

Multiple strategies achieve appropriate humidity. Misting—hand-spraying enclosure surfaces with water—is traditional and allows daily observation. Automated misting systems reduce labor, though they require more sophisticated setup. Substrate moisture—using wet moss or regular substrate watering—passively increases humidity through evaporation. Water features like background waterfalls create humidity while providing visual interest.

Enclosure design fundamentally affects humidity maintenance. Ventilation that's excessive causes rapid moisture loss, while insufficient ventilation promotes mold and fungal growth. Balancing these opposing forces requires trial and adjustment. Sealed glass aquariums trap humidity well, while plastic storage containers can be modified with ventilation holes to achieve appropriate balance.

Substrate Humidity Maintenance

Substrate selection directly impacts moisture retention. Sphagnum moss retains water exceptionally well, supporting humidity while providing cushioning. Coconut husk holds moisture while allowing slow drainage. Potting soil works for terrestrial species but can become anaerobic if too wet. Combining materials—layering drainage material, sphagnum, and top soil—creates stratified substrate that maintains appropriate moisture at different depths.

Water bowls or shallow water features contribute to humidity in semi-aquatic setups. Tree frog enclosures benefit from shallow water dishes where animals can soak, while completely aquatic species like axolotls require full water environments. Designing water features that don't dominate enclosures allows tree frogs and other primarily arboreal species to access water without encouraging excessive aquatic behavior.

Temperature Interaction with Humidity

Temperature and humidity interact critically. Warmer air holds more moisture, so a given humidity level feels different at 70°F versus 80°F. Tropical species requiring both warmth and high humidity present the greatest challenge—heating a humid enclosure without drying it requires careful balance. Conversely, cool-climate species like alpine newts may require less precise humidity maintenance as cool air naturally retains moisture.

Advanced Topics: Breeding, Health, and Long-Term Care

As keepers progress, breeding amphibians and managing complex health issues become relevant. While beyond beginner scope, understanding these topics enhances long-term success.

Breeding Amphibians in Captivity

Many amphibian species breed readily in well-maintained captive conditions. Dart frogs particularly are prolific breeders, with females depositing eggs on moist substrate. Tadpoles develop without intervention, though rearing them requires separate rearing containers and appropriate food (algae wafers, specialized tadpole food, and blanched vegetables).

Successful breeding requires seasonal conditioning—many amphibians breed after experiencing slight temperature and photoperiod changes. Providing dry seasons (slightly reduced watering) followed by heavy rain-like conditions through misting simulates natural breeding triggers.

Common Amphibian Health Issues

Red leg syndrome, characterized by reddening of undersides and legs, indicates bacterial infection. Treatment involves improving water quality, increasing temperature slightly (if appropriate for species), and sometimes antibiotic-medicated water. Fungal infections present as fuzzy white growth on skin and are common in excessively wet conditions. Improving ventilation and drying slightly while maintaining humidity balance usually resolves fungal issues.

Parasites can affect amphibians, particularly newly imported wild-caught animals (though we exclusively sell captive-bred specimens). Quarantine protocols—housing new arrivals separately for 4-6 weeks while monitoring for health issues—prevent introducing parasites to established collections.

Extended Lifespan Care

Many amphibians live substantially longer than commonly expected. White's tree frogs regularly reach 20+ years, while axolotls commonly live 15 years. Managing aging amphibians involves monitoring for declining appetite, observing for behavioral changes, and adjusting environmental parameters to compensate for reduced activity. Many long-term keepers maintain detailed records of their animals, noting changes and patterns that inform ongoing care adjustments.

Shipping and Quarantine: Ensuring Healthy Arrival

We ship all amphibians with insulated packaging and heat/cool packs appropriate to season. Amphibians tolerate shipping well when packaged correctly, arriving within 24-48 hours. Upon arrival, open your animal's enclosure in a dimly lit area, allowing them to acclimate for 2-3 hours before offering food. Most amphibians are slightly stressed post-shipping and may refuse food initially—this is normal.

We recommend a 2-4 week quarantine period before introducing newly arrived amphibians to established collections. House them separately, observe for signs of illness, and ensure they're eating regularly before integration. This prevents introducing pathogens to established animals and allows behavioral adjustment to new environments.
