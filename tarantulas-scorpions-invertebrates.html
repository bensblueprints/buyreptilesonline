Tarantula Care Basics: What You Need to Know Before Buying

Before you purchase tarantulas for sale, understanding fundamental care requirements is essential. Tarantulas are long-lived invertebrates that can thrive in captivity for 15+ years (females) when provided with proper conditions. Unlike mammals or reptiles, tarantulas have relatively simple but specific needs.

Enclosure Requirements

Pet tarantulas need appropriately sized enclosures that balance security with proper space. The general rule is an enclosure with a footprint of approximately 3-4 inches per inch of leg span for most species. Ventilation is critical—tarantulas can develop respiratory issues in overly moist conditions, though some species require higher humidity than others.

Enclosure options include:

  • Clear plastic deli containers with proper ventilation holes
  • Glass terrariums with secure lids
  • Commercial tarantula enclosures designed for optimal visibility and safety

Substrate selection matters significantly for tarantulas for sale. Most species appreciate 2-3 inches of substrate that mimics their natural habitat—this might be dry coco fiber for desert species or moist peat moss for tropical varieties.

Temperature and Humidity

Most pet tarantulas thrive at room temperature (68-72°F). Unlike reptiles, tarantulas generally don't require heating equipment, though warmth accelerates their metabolic rate and growth. Humidity varies by species: New World species typically prefer 60-70% humidity, while Old World species may tolerate drier conditions.

Feeding Your Pet Tarantula

Pet tarantulas are carnivorous invertebrates that feed on appropriately sized prey. Most common options include crickets, roaches, and grasshoppers. Prey size should be roughly 1/3 the size of the tarantula's body. Juvenile tarantulas eat more frequently (every 3-5 days), while adults may feed weekly or even less frequently.

Live feeding is the most common method, though pre-killed prey can work if you're comfortable handling it. Always remove uneaten prey within 24 hours to prevent injury to your pet.

Water and Hydration

Provide a shallow water dish (bottle cap works well) that your tarantula can safely drink from without drowning. Change it regularly to prevent bacterial growth. Some species obtain moisture from their food and humid substrate.

Handling and Safety

Not all pet tarantulas are equally handleable, and even docile species can bite if threatened. Tarantulas have venomous fangs, though their venom is mild to humans—similar to a mosquito bite for most species. Protective urticating hairs found on many New World species can cause skin irritation if you're scratched.

If you're new to tarantulas for sale, choose species known for docile temperaments and develop confidence with handling techniques before attempting interaction with your pet.

Old World vs. New World Tarantulas: Understanding the Differences

When browsing tarantulas for sale, you'll encounter the terms "Old World" and "New World." Understanding this fundamental distinction is crucial for selecting appropriate pet tarantulas for your experience level.

New World Tarantulas

New World tarantulas originate from the Americas and are generally the safer choice for beginners and those handling their pet tarantulas. Defining characteristics include:

  • Urticating hairs: These species have barbed defense hairs on their abdomens that they can kick toward threats, causing mild irritation. This is their preferred defense mechanism.
  • Docile temperament: Most New World species are relatively calm and reluctant to bite.
  • Mild venom: While venomous, their venom is relatively weak and comparable to a bee sting for humans.
  • Slower growth: New World tarantulas generally grow more slowly than their Old World counterparts.
  • Beginner-friendly: Most New World species are excellent choices for those new to keeping pet tarantulas.

Old World Tarantulas

Old World tarantulas hail from Europe, Africa, Asia, and Australia. While undeniably beautiful, they require experienced keepers and present different handling considerations:

  • No urticating hairs: This defense mechanism is absent, making them more likely to bite when threatened.
  • Potent venom: Old World venom is significantly more powerful than New World species, causing more severe pain and potential medical complications.
  • Fast and unpredictable: Many Old World species are lightning-quick and defensive, making them challenging to handle safely.
  • Faster growth: Old World tarantulas often reach maturity more quickly, making them rewarding for patient observers.
  • Advanced care required: These pet tarantulas demand respect and careful handling protocols.

As your knowledge grows and you develop handling skills, exploring Old World tarantulas becomes more feasible. Many collectors maintain both, enjoying the contrasting characteristics of each group.

Handling Your Pet Tarantula: Techniques and Safety

One of the joys of keeping pet tarantulas is the opportunity to interact with and observe these remarkable creatures up close. However, handling requires knowledge and respect for both your safety and your tarantula's wellbeing.

Is Your Tarantula Ready to Handle?

Not all pet tarantulas are equally willing to be handled. Before attempting to pick up your tarantula:

  • Allow acclimation time (at least 1-2 weeks in their new enclosure)
  • Observe their temperament and response to disturbances
  • Ensure they're not in premolt (the period before shedding)
  • Choose calm times, not immediately after feeding
  • Start with brief interactions and increase duration gradually

Safe Handling Techniques

When handling pet tarantulas, use these proven techniques:

The Hand Method: Allow your calm tarantula to walk across your outstretched hands. Move slowly and let them control the pace. Keep your hands flat and close together, preventing escape or falls.

The Container Method: Place your hand flat in the enclosure and allow the tarantula to climb aboard. Immediately remove and observe in a safe space (over a bed or couch, never over hard floors).

Distance Observation: Many keepers enjoy watching pet tarantulas without direct handling, especially for nervous individuals or more defensive species.

Warning Signs and When to Stop

Respect your tarantula's boundaries. Warning signs include:

  • Threat posture (raised front legs, fangs visible)
  • Rapid movements or attempts to flee
  • Hair kicking behavior (particularly on New World species)
  • Aggressive feeding posture
  • Recent molting or obvious signs of premolt

If your tarantula displays these signs, return it to its enclosure immediately. Pet tarantulas that feel threatened may bite, which while rarely dangerous for humans, can harm the tarantula through defensive actions or human response.

Drop Safety

Even well-acclimated pet tarantulas can fall. A drop from hand height can crack their exoskeleton fatally. Always handle over soft surfaces (beds, grass, towels) and never handle over hard floors, tables, or sinks.

Tarantula Molt: Understanding This Critical Life Stage

Molting is a fascinating and sometimes frightening aspect of keeping pet tarantulas. Understanding this process prevents panic and ensures your tarantula's safety.

The Premolt Period

Signs that your pet tarantula is entering premolt include:

  • Loss of appetite or refusal to feed for extended periods
  • Reduced activity and spending more time in their retreat
  • Darkened appearance, particularly on the abdomen
  • Production of a molting mat (a silk pad they rest on during the process)

During premolt, stop handling your pet tarantula entirely. Their exoskeleton is loosening from their body, making them vulnerable. Feeding should also stop, as they cannot safely process food during this period.

The Molt Itself

Molting can take from a few hours to several days. Your tarantula will lie on their back, seemingly immobilized. This appearance is alarming but completely normal. During this time, resist the urge to help or interfere. They're going through an incredibly important biological process.

Post-Molt Care

After your pet tarantula has finished molting, allow at least one week before offering food. Their exoskeleton needs time to harden fully. Resume normal handling only after they've shown normal feeding behavior for several days.

Post-molt molting brings striking changes: colors become more vibrant, patterns sharpen, and your tarantula may appear noticeably larger overnight. This is the most visually rewarding time of the tarantula year.

Scorpions for Sale: Care and Housing Your Arachnid

Scorpion Housing Requirements

Unlike tarantulas, scorpions for sale have specific environmental needs:

  • Enclosure size: A 10-gallon tank works for most species, though larger is beneficial for exploration.
  • Substrate depth: 4-6 inches allows burrowing behavior, which most scorpions appreciate.
  • Humidity: Requirements vary by species, from 60% (desert species) to 80% (forest species).
  • Temperature: 75-82°F is ideal for most scorpions for sale.
  • Hiding spots: Multiple retreats (cork bark, hides) are essential for security and natural behavior.
  • Escape-proof design: Scorpions are escape artists; lids must be secure and gaps sealed.

Feeding Scorpions

Scorpions for sale are carnivorous and feed on insects and small arthropods. Crickets, roaches, and grasshoppers are standard prey items. Most scorpions eat less frequently than tarantulas—feeding every 7-10 days is typical for adults. Juveniles eat more frequently.

Feeding is entertainment value too: watching a scorpion hunt and subdue prey is compelling and provides insight into natural predatory behavior.

Handling and Temperament

Scorpion handling depends heavily on species choice. Emperor Scorpions can be handled gently and regularly, making them good entry-point scorpions for sale. Most other species are best observed rather than handled. Scorpion stings are painful but rarely dangerous for humans (except specific Old World species), though the experience is unpleasant.

Setting Up the Perfect Invertebrate Housing

Whether you're buying tarantulas for sale or exploring other invertebrates for sale, proper housing is fundamental to success. General principles apply across species:

Enclosure Selection

Options vary based on species needs and budget:

  • Plastic deli containers: Affordable, readily available, customizable. Excellent for spiders and small invertebrates for sale.
  • Clear plastic critter cages: Mid-range option with better structural design than containers.
  • Glass terrariums: Attractive, durable, but heavier and more expensive. Better for larger specimens.
  • Bioactive vivariums: Advanced option using live plants and soil cultures for naturalistic setups.

Ventilation Essentials

Proper air circulation prevents mold, fungal growth, and respiratory issues. Most invertebrates for sale benefit from passive ventilation through small, strategic holes. Enclosures should never be completely sealed, though security is equally important.

Lighting Considerations

Most invertebrates for sale are crepuscular or nocturnal and don't require specialized lighting. However, appropriate light/dark cycles (mimicking natural day/night patterns) support natural behavior. Some keepers use low-intensity LED lighting for observation without stressing their invertebrates.

Substrate Selection

Substrate depth, moisture level, and composition vary dramatically by species. Common substrates for invertebrates for sale include:

  • Coco fiber (various moisture levels)
  • Peat moss (moisture-retaining)
  • Sphagnum moss (highly humid)
  • Sand/soil mixes (for burrowing species)
  • Aspen shavings (dry, suitable for desert species)
  • Bark and wood chunks (naturalistic, beneficial)

Temperature and Humidity Management

Most invertebrates for sale thrive at room temperature (68-75°F). Temperature fluctuations stress these animals. Humidity control is species-specific but critical: hygrometers help monitor conditions. Most invertebrates benefit from a humidity gradient—areas of varying moisture within the same enclosure.

Feeding Your Invertebrates: A Complete Guide

Whether you're keeping pet tarantulas, scorpions, or other invertebrates for sale, understanding nutritional needs is crucial.

Prey Selection and Sizing

Standard prey items for invertebrates for sale include:

  • Crickets: Most common, readily available, affordable. Gutload with vegetables before feeding.
  • Roaches: Nutritious, less noise than crickets, readily available in various sizes.
  • Grasshoppers: Excellent prey, particularly for larger invertebrates for sale.
  • Fruit flies and gnats: Ideal for jumping spiders and small species.
  • Mealworms: Supplementary option, less nutritious than other choices.

Prey sizing is critical: appropriate sizes are roughly 1/3 of your pet's body size. Too large, and your tarantula, scorpion, or other invertebrate may struggle. Too small, and they're inefficient food.

Feeding Frequency

Feeding schedules vary by species, age, and individual metabolism:

  • Juvenile tarantulas: Every 3-5 days
  • Adult tarantulas: Weekly to every 10 days
  • Scorpions: Every 7-10 days
  • Jumping spiders: Every 2-3 days
  • Centipedes: Every 5-7 days
  • Millipedes: Daily access to vegetables and fruits

Many invertebrates for sale actually prefer less frequent feeding than typical schedules suggest. Observe your specimens and adjust accordingly.

Gutloading Prey

Before offering prey to your invertebrates for sale, gutload them (feed nutritious food to prey insects 24 hours before presentation). This increases nutritional value transferred to your pets. Offer prey items vegetables, fruits, and high-quality insect diet.

Removing Uneaten Prey

Always remove uneaten prey within 24 hours. Living prey can injure your invertebrates for sale, particularly during molting or vulnerable periods.

Health Monitoring and Common Issues

Keeping invertebrates for sale healthy requires vigilance and understanding of common problems.

Signs of Healthy Invertebrates

Your pet tarantulas, scorpions, and other invertebrates for sale should exhibit:

  • Active behavior appropriate to their species
  • Regular feeding responses
  • Clean, unblemished exoskeletons
  • Clear, responsive movements
  • Appropriate humidity and temperature maintenance

Common Health Problems

Mites: Parasitic mites sometimes infest invertebrates for sale. Signs include visible mites and lethargy. Prevention through enclosure cleanliness is essential; treatment may require professional guidance.

Fungal and Bacterial Infections: Excessive moisture can promote fungal growth. Improve ventilation and substrate management if you notice discoloration or unusual odors.

Refused Feeding: If your tarantulas or other invertebrates for sale stop eating, check for premolt signs, stress, or temperature extremes.

Dehydration: Ensure water sources are available. Some invertebrates for sale need humidity; others prefer dry conditions. Match species-specific requirements.

When to Seek Professional Help

Veterinarians experienced with exotic invertebrates are rare but exist. Contact local exotic pet vets or universities if you're concerned about serious health issues in your invertebrates for sale.
